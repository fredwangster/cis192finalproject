	<p>If you have a mental or physical condition that keeps you from working you could qualify for Social Security Disability (also called SSI or SSDI). The Social Security Administration refers to work as "substantial gainful activity." This is just another way of saying any type of work or hobby that earns you money.</p>
	<p>An experienced social security advocate or attorney can help you determine the programs for which you are qualified. To receive Social Security Disability there are five typical questions that attorneys, advocates and the SSA may ask to start to determine your eligibility:</p>
	&nbsp 1. <b>Have you worked for five of the last ten years?</b><br />
	<ul>
	<li>All applicants between the ages of 31 and 64 must meet this requirement. You need not have worked 5 consecutive years, but you must have worked for more than five total in the past ten years. </li>
	<li>If you have been disabled BEFORE the age of 24, the law requires that you have worked 1.5 years of the three years.</li>
	<li>For those disabled between the ages of 24 and 30 the law requires that you have worked at least half of the time from your 21st birthday to the date of your disability.</li>
	</ul><br />
	&nbsp 2. <b>Has your disability lasted or is it expected to last longer than 12 months?</b>
	<ul>
	<li>The SSA refers to this as the "durational requirement." It means that you have been or expect to be disabled for at least 12 months or may result in death. </li>
	</ul>
	&nbsp 3. <b>Are you under the age of 65?</b><br /><br />
	&nbsp 4. <b>Are you unable to perform "substantial gainful activity"?</b>
	<ul>
	<li>Performing any type of activity that earns you money or increases your potential to make money may disqualify you from receiving benefits. These activities include hobbies, school attendance, and volunteer work. </li>
	</ul>
	&nbsp 5. <b>Can you provide a doctor's diagnosis and medical evidence that supports your disability claim?</b>
	<ul>
	<li>Medical evidence of your disability must demonstrate that you are suffering an anatomical, physiological or psychological condition that prevents you from performing ANY type of work.</li>
	<li>If your condition is on the SSA's list of impairments that qualify for disability benefits, and your medical evidence clearly shows that you meet the criteria for that impairment, your application has a strong chance of being approved.</li>
	</ul>
	<p>The law is extensive, and if you do not meet some of these requirements you may still qualify or you may qualify for Supplemental Security Income (also called SSI). Some physical and mental conditions that keep you from working are difficult to diagnose with objective testing, so an advocate or attorney can help you present your doctor's reports properly. Their help can assist you in demonstrating to the government that you deserve benefits. </p>

	<span class="redbold">SOCIAL SECURITY DEFINED</span><br />
	<ol start="1">
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('1');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>What is Social Security?</li></a></b>
	<div id="1" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> The Social Security Disability Insurance program(SSDI) pays benefits to people (or certain family members) who cannot work because they have a medical condition that is expected to last at least one year or result in death. Federal law requires this very strict definition of disability. While some programs give money to people with partial disability or short-term disability, Social Security does not. To qualify, you must have worked a certain period of time and paid Social Security taxes.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('2');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>What is Supplemental Security Income (SSI)?</li></a></b>
	<div id="2" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> The SSI program makes payments to people with low income who are age 65 or older, are blind, or have a disability. The Social Security Administration manages the SSI program. Even though Social Security manages the program, SSI is not paid for by Social Security taxes. SSI is paid for by U.S. Treasury general funds, not the Social Security trust funds.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('3');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>How does the Social Security Act define "disability"?</li></a></b>
	<div id="3" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> For all individuals applying for disability benefits under the Social Security Disability Insurance program (Title II), and for adults applying under the Supplemental Security Income program (Title XVI), the definition of disability is the same. The law defines disability as the inability to engage in any substantial gainful activity (SGA) by reason of any medically determinable physical or mental impairment(s) which can be expected to result in death or which has lasted or can be expected to last for a continuous period of not less than 12 months.<br /><br />
	Under title XVI, a child under age 18 will be considered disabled if he or she has a medically determinable physical or mental impairment or combination of impairments that causes marked and severe functional limitations, and that can be expected to cause death or that has lasted or can be expected to last for a continuous period of not less than 12 months.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('4');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>Who makes the initial Social Security Disability Insurance or Supplemental Security Income decision?</li></a></b>
	<div id="4" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> At the initial stage of approval a DDS examiner makes the disability determination decision. The DDS is the state-level agency which decides claims that are filed through the Social Security Administration. A DDS Examiner Level II also makes the reconsideration level decision. However, at the third stage, a hearing is held before an Administrative Law Judge who will make the decision after hearing testimony.<br /><br /></div></ol>
	<span class="redbold">QUALIFYING FOR BENEFITS</span><br />
	<ol start="5">
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('5');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>How do I know if I will qualify to receive Social Security disability benefits?</li></a></b>
	<div id="5" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> There is no way to know with 100% certainty whether your will qualify for benefits. However, if you are unable to perform gainful work due to a disability, and/or your disability is included on the SSA List of Impairments, then you may qualify. An experienced <a href="javascript:displayForm('1');" class="underlinedredtext">advocate or attorney</a> will be able to review your claim and provide you with a general idea of how your evidence supports your case. Unfortunately, until your claim has been approved, there are no guarantees.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('6');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>Do I have to be permanently disabled to qualify for Social Security disability benefits?</li></a></b>
	<div id="6" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> No, you do not have to be permanently disabled. To qualify for Social Security disability benefits you must have a disabling condition that has lasted, or will last, for longer than 12 months or cause death within a year.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('7');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>How does the Social Security Administration decide if I can receive disability benefits? </li></a></b>
	<div id="7" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> The SSA uses a five-step process to decide if you are disabled.<br /><BR />
	<b>1.	Are you working?</b><br />
	If you are working and your earnings average more than a certain amount each month (currently $980/month), they generally will not consider you disabled. The amount changes each year. For the updated figure, see the annual Update (Publication No. 05-10003).<br /><br />
	If you are not working, or your monthly earnings average the current amount or less, the state agency then looks at your medical condition.<br /><br />
	<b>2.	Is your medical condition "severe"?</b><br />
	For the state agency to decide that you are disabled, your medical condition must significantly limit your ability to do basic work activities-such as walking, sitting and remembering-for at least one year. If your medical condition is not that severe, the state agency will not consider you disabled. If your condition is that severe, the state agency goes on to step three.<br /><br />
	<b>3.	Is your medical condition on the List of Impairments?</b><br />
	The state agency has a List of Impairments that describes medical conditions that are considered so severe that they automatically mean that you are disabled as defined by law. If your condition (or combination of medical conditions) is not on this list, the state agency looks to see if your condition is as severe as a condition that is on the list. If the severity of your medical condition meets or equals that of a listed impairment, the state agency will decide that you are disabled. If it does not, the state agency goes on to step four.<br /><br />
	<b>4.	Can you do the work you did before?</b><br />
	At this step, the state agency decides if your medical condition prevents you from being able to do the work you did before. If it does not, the state agency will decide that you are not disabled. If it does, the state agency goes on to step five.<br /><br />
	<b>5.	Can you do any other type of work?</b><br />
	If you cannot do the work you did in the past, the state agency looks to see if you would be able to do other work. It evaluates your medical condition, your age, education, past work experience and any skills you may have that could be used to do other work. If you cannot do other work, the state agency will decide that you are disabled. If you can do other work, the state agency will decide that you are not disabled.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('8');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>Can I work and receive benefits?</li></a></b>
	<div id="8" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> After you start receiving disability benefits, you may want to try working again. There are special rules that help you keep your cash benefits and Medicare while you test your ability to work. These rules are called "work incentives" or "employment support" programs. <br /><br />
	During the trial work period, there are no limits on your �earnings. During the 36-month extended period of eligibility, you usually can make no more than $980 a month or your benefits will stop. But, the work expenses you have as a result of your disability are deducted when they count your earnings, to see if they can help you keep more of your benefits. If you have extra work expenses, your earnings could be substantially higher than $980 before they affect your benefits. This substantial earnings amount usually increases each year.<br /><br />
	They deduct work expenses related to your disability from your earnings before the determine if you are still eligible for benefits. These expenses may include the cost of any item or service you need to work, even if the item or service also is useful to you in your daily living. Examples include prescription drugs, transportation to and from work (under certain conditions), a personal attendant or job coach, a wheelchair or any specialized work equipment.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('9');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>If I am disabled, does my family qualify for Social Security disability benefits?</li></a></b>
	<div id="9" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> Certain members of your family may qualify for benefits based on your work. They include:<br />
	<ul>
	<li>Your spouse, if he or she is 62 or older;</li>
	<li>Your spouse, at any age if he or she is caring for a child of yours who is younger than age 16 or disabled;</li>
	<li>Your unmarried child, including an adopted child, or, in some cases, a stepchild or grandchild. The child must be under age 18 or under age 19 if in elementary or secondary school full time; and</li>
	<li>Your unmarried child, age 18 or older, if he or she has a disability that started before age 22. (The child's disability also must meet the definition of disability for adults.)</li>
	</ul>
	NOTE: In some situations, a divorced spouse may qualify for benefits based on your earnings if he or she was married to you for at least 10 years, is not currently married and is at least age 62. The money paid to a divorced spouse does not reduce your benefit or any benefits due to your current spouse or children. <br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('10');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>I am presently a stay-at-home parent, but I used to work outside of the home. Can I qualify for Social Security disability benefits if I become sick or injured?</li></a></b>
	<div id="10" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> Maybe. If you have worked 5 out of the last 10 years before becoming disabled, or if you're under 31 and worked before becoming disabled you may qualify for benefits. If your earnings are low enough since you've been an at-home parent, then you may qualify for the Supplemental Security Income program (SSI). <br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('11');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>I have never worked at public work, but am disabled. Would I qualify for Social Security disability benefits if I become sick or injured?</li></a></b>
	<div id="11" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> Maybe. You may qualify for Supplemental Security Income (SSI), which is different from the Social Security Disability Insurance(SSDI) program. The SSI program doesn't consider a person's work history or earnings record.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('12');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>I am a disabled widow, and I haven't worked at public work for many years. Can I receive Social Security disability benefits?</li></a></b>
	<div id="12" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> Maybe. If you're over 50 and became disabled within 7 years of your spouse's death, then you may qualify for Disabled Widow's benefits. You may also qualify if you are over 50 and became disabled within 7 years of your last check from your parent's Social Security benefits. Another way you may qualify is if your earnings and possessions are low enough to create eligibility for SSI benefits. 	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('58');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>What are my chances of getting my claim approved after an appeal hearing with an Administrative Law Judge?</li></a></b>
	<div id="58" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> Statistics about win rates at the appeal and hearing levels are hard to find, however many attorneys report win rates of 60-80% for all cases they take to a hearing. However, not all cases that are not approved at lower levels are candidates for taking to a hearing.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('59');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>Can I appeal further if my claim is denied after reconsideration?</li></a></b>
	<div id="59" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> If you disagree with the reconsideration decision, you may ask for a hearing. An Administrative Law Judge who was not involved in the original decision or the reconsideration of your case will conduct the hearing. The hearing is usually held within 75 miles of your home. The administrative law judge will notify you of the time and place of the hearing.<br /><br />
	Before the hearing, you may be asked to give more evidence and to clarify information about your claim. You may look at the information in your file and give new information.<br /><br />
	At the hearing, the administrative law judge will question you and any witnesses you bring. Other witnesses, such as medical or vocational experts, also may give us information at the hearing. You or your representative may question the witnesses.<br /><br />
	In certain situations, we may hold your hearing by a videoconference rather than in person. You will be notified ahead of time if this is the case. With video hearings, the SSA can make the hearing more convenient for you. Often an appearance by video hearing can be scheduled faster than an in-person appearance. Also, a video hearing location may be closer to your home. That might make it easier for you to have witnesses or other people accompany you.<br /><br />
	It is usually to your advantage to attend the hearing (in person or video conference). You and your representative, if you have one, should come to the hearing and explain your case.<br /><br />
	If you are unable to attend a hearing or do not wish to do so, you must explain this in writing as soon as you can. Unless the administrative law judge believes your presence is necessary to decide your case and requires you to attend, you will not have to go. Or they may be able to make other arrangements for you, such as changing the time or place of your hearing. You have to have a good reason for them to make other arrangements.<br /><br />
	After the hearing, the judge will make a decision based on all the information in your case, including any new information you give. You will be sent a letter and a copy of the judge's decision.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('60');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>What is the Appeals Council?</li></a></b>
	<div id="60" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> The Appeals Council is the reviewing board to Administrative Law Judge decisions.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('61');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>How soon can I get a hearing on my claim?</li></a></b>
	<div id="61" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> It can take anywhere from 3 months to a year after you request a hearing to get your hearing scheduled depending on the backlog of cases in your area. Learn more here about the social security backlog.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('62');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>What are my chances of getting my claim approved at the hearing stage?</li></a></b>
	<div id="62" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> Statistics about win rates at the appeal and hearing levels are hard to find, however many attorneys report win rates of 60-80% for all cases they take to a hearing.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('63');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>Can I appeal my case to the Federal Court?</li></a></b>
	<div id="63" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> If the Appeals Council denies your claim, it is possible for you to file a civil action suit in the U.S. District Court to review Social Security's decision. Although rare, every one to two years, the U.S. Supreme Court will hear a Social Security disability case appeal.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('64');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>What can I do if Social Security notifies me that they are going to cut off my benefits?</li></a></b>
	<div id="64" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> You can appeal the SSA's decision to end your disability benefits within 10 days of receiving the notification letter. If you do not appeal in that timeframe you might lose your right to appeal.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('65');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>Does Social Security continue to review my case to see if I am still disabled?</li></a></b>
	<div id="65" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> Yes. Your case will be reviewed at regular intervals to make sure you are still disabled.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('66');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>My doctor has verified that I am disabled, but Social Security has denied my claim - why?</li></a></b>
	<div id="66" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> Social Security makes a determination of your disability independently of your doctor's position. The SSA has a very specific definition of what constitutes a disability and requires "medically acceptable clinical and laboratory findings" as evidence. Many disabling conditions are difficult to diagnose objectively. Additionally, your doctor may define a disability in a different manner than the SSA.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('67');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>The VA has determined that I am disabled, so why has the Social Security Administration denied my claim?</li></a></b>
	<div id="67" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> The VA may consider a condition a disability, where the SSA definition will result in a different decision for that same condition. Also, VA decisions are not binding on Social Security.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('68');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>I am disabled and need help with my medical bills more than I need cash income. How can I get help for these medical bills?</li></a></b>
	<div id="68" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> In order to receive help with medical bills, you need to have received approval on a Social Security disability claim. Help with medical bills will begin after you begin to receive your cash benefits. These two benefits are tied together.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('69');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>If I get Social Security disability benefits, can I still receive Medicare?</li></a></b>
	<div id="69" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> Yes, after you are approved for SSDI benefits, you will receive automatic Medicare coverage two years after Social Security deems that your disability began.<br /><br /></div>
	<b><a href="javascript:displaydiv('70');" onclick="void(0);" class="underlinedblackboldtext"><li>Will my Social Security disability benefits prevent me from receiving Medicaid?</li></a></b>
	<div id="70" style="display:none; padding-top:5px;" class="underlinedbluetext"><b>Answer:</b> No, it is possible to get both Medicare and Medicaid. If you receive Supplemental Security Income, you will get Medicaid. You may at the same time qualify for other types of disability benefits from Social Security at the same time.</div></ol>
